Should We Separate Art from the Artist?

By Dr. Jonathan Feldman, Ph.D. in Aesthetics & Art Philosophy

Published: March 18, 2025

For centuries, the question of whether we should separate art from the artist has sparked intense debate...

Art as an Independent Entity

Art, once created, acquires an independent existence. This perspective aligns with postmodernist theories, notably Roland Barthes' concept of "The Death of the Author," which posits that the creator's intentions and personal history should not confine the interpretation of their work. Instead, meaning is generated through the audience's engagement with the piece, allowing art to stand apart from its creator's personal attributes or actions.

Art, once created, belongs to the public consciousness. The artist may have given it life, but its meaning is shaped by those who engage with it.

Historical Precedent

Throughout history, many influential artists have had personal lives or beliefs that were controversial or morally questionable. Despite this, their works have been celebrated and studied for their artistic merit. For instance, Richard Wagner's contributions to music are acknowledged independently of his personal views. This suggests a longstanding recognition of the value in assessing art based on its own qualities rather than the character of its creator.​

Preservation of Cultural Heritage

Dismissing all art created by individuals with flawed histories risks losing significant cultural and historical artifacts. Art serves as a reflection of its time, capturing societal values, struggles, and achievements. By separating art from the artist, valuable cultural touchstones remain accessible for future generations.

Psychological Engagement with Art

Studies indicate that negative information about an artist can influence audience responses to their work. For example, research published in Frontiers in Psychology found that paintings by artists associated with negative biographical information were liked less and found more arousing than those associated with neutral information. However, evaluative judgments of quality were not affected, suggesting that while emotional responses are influenced, the perceived quality of art can remain separate from the artist's personal history.
